Living Room Design Trends Dominating Indian Homes in 2025

From Japandi calm to maximalist warmth — we mapped the shifts across 200+ homes to bring you what's actually defining how India lives in 2025.

GHAR.tv Design Studio Design Team
March 20, 2026 14 min read Updated Q1 2026

Key Takeaways

  • Japandi (Japanese-Scandinavian fusion) is the dominant aesthetic in 42% of new luxury interiors
  • Earthy palettes — terracotta, sage green, warm beige — have replaced the grey-white dominance of the 2018–2022 era
  • Statement lighting has become the centrepiece of living room design, with pendant lights replacing chandeliers
  • Handcrafted and artisanal furniture from Indian makers is seeing a 3x demand surge over mass-produced imports
  • Open-plan living rooms integrated with dining and kitchen zones now account for 68% of new premium apartment layouts
01

The Japandi Revolution

Walk into any recently designed living room in Bengaluru, Mumbai, or Hyderabad and you will likely notice something distinct: the furniture sits lower, the palette runs quieter, and there is an almost meditative sense of space that earlier generations of Indian interiors rarely pursued. This is Japandi — a design philosophy that fuses the clean minimalism of Japanese interiors with the organic warmth of Scandinavian living. In our survey of over 200 recently completed homes across eight Indian metros, 42% of luxury interiors now explicitly reference Japandi as their primary design language, up from just 11% in 2022.

The appeal of Japandi in the Indian context goes deeper than aesthetics. Japanese design has always valued the concept of wabi-sabi — finding beauty in imperfection and transience — which resonates strongly with Indian craft traditions where the hand of the maker is visible in every piece. Scandinavian hygge, with its emphasis on cosy comfort and lived-in warmth, translates naturally to the Indian family room where multiple generations gather. The fusion works because it marries restraint with warmth, minimalism with soul — something that neither purely Japanese nor purely Scandinavian interiors achieve on their own.

42% of new luxury interiors adopt Japandi aesthetics

In practice, Japandi living rooms in Indian homes feature low-profile sofas (typically 35–40 cm seat height versus the traditional 45–50 cm), natural materials like oak, walnut, and rattan, and a deliberately restrained colour palette of off-whites, warm greys, and muted greens. The key Indian adaptation is the integration of locally sourced materials — Rajasthani sandstone side tables, Kerala teak flooring, and handwoven dhurrie rugs from Jaipur — that ground the global aesthetic in a distinctly Indian materiality. Designers report that clients are increasingly requesting spaces that feel both contemporary and rooted, global yet unmistakably Indian.

02

The Earthy Palette Shift

The colour revolution in Indian living rooms is perhaps the most visible trend of 2025. After nearly five years of grey-white dominance — driven partly by the Scandinavian minimalism wave and partly by the Instagram-friendly all-white aesthetic — Indian homeowners are embracing warmth with a conviction that signals a lasting shift rather than a passing fad. Terracotta, sage green, warm beige, dusty rose, and charcoal have emerged as the defining palette of this new era, and the reasons are as practical as they are aesthetic.

India's abundant natural light, which can wash out cool greys and make pure whites feel clinical, actually enhances earthy tones. A terracotta accent wall in a south-facing Mumbai apartment glows with depth through the day as sunlight shifts, while the same wall in cool grey would flatten into monotony by noon. Sage green pairs naturally with the tropical foliage visible through most Indian windows, creating a visual continuity between interior and exterior that cool neutrals never achieved. Interior designers across the country report that clients who once asked for all-white kitchens are now requesting warm beige cabinetry with brass hardware — a combination that hides wear better while looking richer. The climate factor cannot be overstated. In a country where temperatures regularly exceed 35°C for months, earthy tones psychologically evoke coolness and groundedness in a way that stark whites — which bounce and amplify harsh light — simply do not. This is why terracotta, which absorbs and softens light, has become the default accent colour in cities from Ahmedabad to Chennai. The trend also aligns with a broader cultural reclamation: these are the colours of Indian earth, Indian clay, and Indian textiles, and their ascendance in interior design mirrors a growing confidence in indigenous aesthetics over imported ones.

03

Statement Lighting as Art

If the living room is a stage, then lighting has become its lead performer. The traditional Indian reliance on a single central chandelier or a ceiling fan with an integrated light kit is being replaced by layered lighting schemes where one or more statement fixtures serve as sculptural focal points. Pendant light clusters — typically three to five pendants hung at varying heights over a coffee table or seating area — have become the signature lighting move of 2025, appearing in nearly half of the professionally designed living rooms we surveyed.

The shift is driven by both design philosophy and technology. LED advances have made it possible to create fixtures that are slim, energy-efficient, and dramatically shaped without the heat and bulk constraints of traditional bulbs. Indian lighting designers and boutique studios — names like Klove, Objectry, and Sahil & Sarthak — are producing globally competitive sculptural lights in materials like blown glass, hand-beaten brass, and woven bamboo that double as art installations. The demand for these designer pieces has grown threefold since 2022, reflecting a willingness among Indian homeowners to invest in lighting as a defining design element rather than an afterthought.

3x growth in designer pendant light demand since 2022

Beyond the hero fixture, the modern Indian living room now incorporates ambient LED strips recessed into false ceilings or shelving units, sculptural floor lamps that anchor reading corners, and smart dimmer systems that allow homeowners to shift the room's mood from bright and social to warm and intimate with a single touch.

The key layers of a well-lit Indian living room in 2025:

  • Ambient layer — recessed LED strips in false ceilings or cove lighting for overall room glow
  • Task layer — focused reading lamps, under-cabinet lighting, or desk lights for functional activities
  • Accent layer — wall washers, track lights, or picture lights to highlight art, textures, or architectural features
  • Statement layer — one or more sculptural pendant fixtures that serve as visual anchors and conversation starters
  • Smart control — dimmers and tunable white LEDs (2700K–5000K) that shift colour temperature from cool daytime to warm evening modes

Dimmer systems, once considered a luxury, are now standard in living rooms budgeted above ₹5 lakh for interiors. The most sophisticated installations use tunable white LEDs that can shift colour temperature from a cool 5000K during daytime work to a warm 2700K for evening relaxation — a feature that aligns with India's growing awareness of how light affects wellbeing.

04

The Handcrafted Furniture Revival

Perhaps the most culturally significant trend in Indian living room design is the resurgence of handcrafted, artisanal furniture. After two decades where mass-produced imports from IKEA, UrbanLadder, and Pepperfry dominated the market with their affordability and convenience, a growing segment of Indian homeowners is actively seeking out pieces made by skilled craftspeople using traditional techniques. This is not merely nostalgia — it represents a fundamental recalibration of what Indian consumers value in their homes: uniqueness over uniformity, story over speed, and durability over disposability.

The numbers bear this out. Searches for handcrafted furniture on Indian e-commerce platforms have tripled since 2022. Direct-to-consumer brands like Phantom Hands (Bengaluru), Jaipur Rugs, and The Wishing Chair have reported revenue growth exceeding 80% year-on-year. Meanwhile, platforms like Okhai and GoCoop are connecting urban buyers directly with rural artisan clusters in Kutch, Saharanpur, Jodhpur, and Channapatna — communities that possess centuries of woodworking, weaving, and metalworking expertise that was at risk of dying out without market access.

The convergence of Indian craft traditions with modern design sensibility is producing remarkable results. A sheesham wood coffee table with clean, contemporary lines but a hand-chiseled surface that reveals the grain in unexpected ways. A cane armchair whose weave pattern references traditional Assamese basket-making but whose silhouette would sit comfortably in a Copenhagen living room.

How to start buying handcrafted furniture for your living room:

  1. Identify one or two anchor pieces — a coffee table and an accent chair are ideal starting points
  2. Research artisan clusters relevant to your material preference (Jodhpur for wood, Kutch for textiles, Channapatna for lacquerware)
  3. Visit platforms like Okhai, GoCoop, or Phantom Hands to compare styles and pricing
  4. Request material samples and finish options before committing — natural wood grains vary significantly
  5. Budget 2–5x more than mass-produced equivalents, but factor in 25–40 year lifespans vs 5–8 years for factory furniture

These pieces cost more than their mass-produced equivalents — often two to five times as much — but they age beautifully, carry a provenance that sparks conversation, and support livelihoods in communities that need it most. For a generation of Indian homeowners who grew up with disposable furniture, investing in pieces that will outlast them feels like a meaningful shift in values.

05

Open-Plan Living Rooms

The walls are coming down — literally. In 68% of new premium apartment layouts across India's top eight cities, the living room now flows seamlessly into the dining area and kitchen, creating a unified social space that reflects how Indian families actually use their homes. The traditional compartmentalised floor plan — with its separate drawing room, dining room, and kitchen connected by narrow corridors — was designed for an era of formal entertaining and kitchen-as-workspace. Today's Indian home revolves around casual togetherness: parents cooking while children study at the dining table, guests chatting across the kitchen island rather than being sequestered in a formal sitting room.

The challenge, of course, is that Indian cooking generates more smoke, steam, and aromatic intensity than the Western cuisines that open-plan living was originally designed around. The solution set has matured considerably. High-suction chimneys (1200–1500 m³/hr), glass partition sliders that can be closed during heavy cooking and opened when entertaining, and strategic zone placement — positioning the cooking hob away from the living area with the sink and prep counter facing outward — have largely solved the odour problem. Some architects are even incorporating a small enclosed utility kitchen behind the main open kitchen, allowing the daily tadka and deep-frying to happen in a ventilated space while the open kitchen handles lighter cooking and serving.

The open-plan trend is also reshaping furniture choices. Modular sofas that can be reconfigured for different social scenarios, extendable dining tables that double as work desks, and kitchen islands with integrated seating have become staples. The visual key to a successful open plan is consistency — a unified flooring material (large-format vitrified tiles are most popular), a cohesive colour palette across zones, and lighting that differentiates areas without creating hard boundaries. Done well, an open-plan Indian living room feels expansive, connected, and genuinely liveable in a way that the compartmentalised floor plan never quite managed.

06

Budget-Wise Implementation

The most common question we receive after publishing trend pieces is straightforward: how much will this actually cost me? The answer varies enormously depending on scope, but the good news is that the most impactful design trends of 2025 are accessible at every budget tier. A ₹1.5 lakh refresh and a ₹15 lakh transformation can both incorporate Japandi sensibility, earthy palettes, and statement lighting — the difference lies in material quality and degree of customisation, not in the fundamental design principles.

At the entry level (under ₹2 lakh), the highest-impact changes are paint and lighting. Repainting your living room walls in a warm earthy tone — terracotta accent wall with warm beige surrounds, for example — costs between ₹15,000 and ₹30,000 for a standard room and immediately shifts the entire character of the space. Replacing a single central light with a cluster of two or three pendant lights from a brand like Jainsons or Corvi can be done for ₹8,000–25,000 and creates a designer look at a fraction of the custom cost. Adding a dimmer switch (₹1,500–5,000) transforms how the room feels at night. These three changes — paint, pendants, and dimmers — deliver roughly 60% of the visual impact for under 15% of a full renovation budget.

Pro Tip

Start with paint colours and lighting before investing in furniture — these two changes transform a room most dramatically for the least cost. A ₹30,000 paint job and ₹15,000 pendant light cluster will change how a room feels more than a ₹2 lakh sofa in the same old setting.

The mid-range tier (₹2–5 lakh) allows for furniture upgrades alongside paint and lighting. This budget typically covers a new sofa or sofa set from a quality domestic brand, a handcrafted coffee table, updated curtains and cushions in earthy textiles, and potentially a feature wall treatment like fluted wood panelling or a limewash finish. For ₹5–10 lakh, homeowners can undertake a comprehensive redesign including custom cabinetry, upgraded flooring, designer lighting throughout, and bespoke furniture from artisan studios. Above ₹10 lakh, the scope extends to structural changes like wall removal for open-plan conversion, premium Italian or Indian marble accents, smart home lighting integration, and fully custom furniture designed specifically for the space. At every tier, the principle holds: invest in what you see and touch most — the sofa, the main light, the wall colour — and save on what recedes into the background.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the most popular living room style in India in 2025?

Japandi — a fusion of Japanese minimalism and Scandinavian warmth — is the dominant aesthetic in 42% of new luxury interiors across India in 2025. This style is often blended with earthy Indian elements like terracotta accents, handwoven textiles, and locally sourced wood to create spaces that feel both global and deeply rooted in Indian sensibility. The style favours low-profile furniture, natural materials, muted tones, and a deliberate sense of openness and calm.

How much does a living room makeover cost in India?

A living room makeover in India can range from ₹1.5 lakh for a basic refresh involving new paint, soft furnishings, and lighting upgrades, to ₹15 lakh or more for a complete transformation with custom furniture, premium flooring, architectural modifications, and designer lighting installations. The mid-range sweet spot of ₹3–7 lakh covers most substantial redesigns including a new sofa, statement lighting, updated colour palette, and feature wall treatment.

Is open-plan living suitable for Indian homes?

Yes, open-plan living is highly suitable for Indian homes when implemented with proper ventilation solutions. A high-powered chimney (minimum 1200 m³/hr suction), glass partition sliders between the kitchen and living area, and strategic placement of the cooking zone away from the main living space address the primary concern of cooking odours. Some homes also incorporate a small enclosed utility kitchen for heavy-duty cooking like frying and tempering, while keeping the main open kitchen for lighter preparation and socialising.

What colours are trending for Indian living rooms in 2025?

Earthy tones have comprehensively replaced the grey-white palette that dominated from 2018 to 2022. The top trending colours are terracotta (38% of new projects), sage green (31%), warm beige (27%), dusty rose (19%), and charcoal (15%). These colours work particularly well with India's abundant natural light and high temperatures, as they absorb and soften harsh sunlight rather than bouncing it around the room. They also complement traditional Indian decor elements like brass, natural wood, and handwoven textiles.
