LEGAL CONSUMER PROTECTION

What Is RERA and How It Protects Buyers

The Real Estate (Regulation and Development) Act, 2016 fundamentally changed the power dynamic between developers and home buyers. This guide explains every protection RERA provides.

GHAR.tv Research Intelligence Team
March 10, 2026 11 min read Updated Q1 2026

Key Takeaways

  • RERA requires registration and full disclosure before any project can be marketed or sold
  • 70% of buyer payments must be deposited in a separate escrow account
  • Buyers can file complaints directly on their state RERA portal
  • Developers are liable for structural defects for 5 years after possession
  • Carpet area must be clearly stated — no ambiguity on super built-up area
01

What Is RERA

The Real Estate (Regulation and Development) Act, 2016, commonly known as RERA, is a landmark legislation enacted by the Indian Parliament to regulate the real estate sector and protect the interests of home buyers. Before RERA, the Indian real estate market was largely unregulated, leading to delayed projects, fund diversion, misleading advertisements, and a significant trust deficit between buyers and developers.

1,10,000+ Projects registered under RERA across India since 2017

RERA established Real Estate Regulatory Authorities in every state and union territory to oversee real estate transactions, adjudicate disputes, and ensure that developers adhere to commitments made to buyers. The Act applies to both residential and commercial projects, covering new launches as well as ongoing projects that had not received a completion certificate at the time of implementation.

03

Developer Obligations Under RERA

RERA places specific, enforceable obligations on developers and promoters. Failure to comply can result in penalties, project deregistration, or even imprisonment. Here are the eight key obligations every developer must meet.

  1. Mandatory registration — Every project above 500 sq. metres or 8 units must be registered with the state RERA authority before any marketing or sales activity.
  2. Escrow compliance — Deposit 70% of all amounts received from buyers into a dedicated escrow account, withdrawable only in proportion to construction progress.
  3. Quarterly project updates — Upload updated project status including construction progress, approvals obtained, and revised timelines every quarter.
  4. Carpet area disclosure — Sell units based on carpet area only and clearly disclose the carpet area in all agreements and marketing material.
  5. No advance without agreement — Cannot collect more than 10% of the unit cost as advance before executing a registered agreement for sale.
  6. Title representation — Provide a legally valid title report and disclose all encumbrances, pending litigations, and rights related to the land.
  7. Structural defect liability — Rectify any structural defect or quality issue reported within 5 years of possession, at no cost to the buyer, within 30 days.
  8. Completion and handover — Obtain completion and occupancy certificates and hand over possession with all promised amenities within the agreed timeline.

Developers who fail to meet these obligations face penalties of up to 10% of the estimated project cost, and in cases of persistent non-compliance, imprisonment of up to 3 years.

04

How to File a RERA Complaint

One of the most empowering aspects of RERA is the ability for buyers to directly file complaints against developers through their state RERA portal. The process is straightforward and can be completed entirely online in most states.

  1. 1
    Visit your state RERA portal

    Go to the official RERA website for the state where the project is registered. Each state has its own portal with an online complaint filing system.

  2. 2
    Register as a complainant

    Create an account on the portal using your personal details, email address, and mobile number. You will receive a verification OTP to activate your account.

  3. 3
    File complaint with RERA number

    Select the complaint type, enter the project RERA registration number, and describe your grievance in detail. Include the developer name, project name, and unit number.

  4. 4
    Upload supporting documents

    Attach copies of your agreement for sale, payment receipts, correspondence with the developer, and any other relevant documentation to support your complaint.

  5. 5
    Track status online

    After submission, you will receive a complaint number. Use this to track hearing dates, orders, and the resolution status directly on the portal.

Documentation Checklist

Before filing, gather these documents: RERA registration number of the project, executed agreement for sale, all payment receipts and bank statements, email or written correspondence with the developer, and any marketing brochures or advertisements that contain commitments.

06

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Despite RERA protections, many buyers still make avoidable mistakes when purchasing property. Being aware of these pitfalls can save you from significant financial and legal trouble.

Never Buy from Non-RERA Projects

Purchasing a unit in an unregistered project means you have no RERA protections whatsoever. Always verify the RERA registration number on your state portal before making any payment.

  • Not verifying the RERA registration number on the state portal before booking
  • Paying more than 10% advance before executing a registered agreement for sale
  • Ignoring the quarterly progress updates published on the RERA portal
  • Accepting verbal promises instead of written, RERA-compliant agreements
  • Not checking the developer’s track record and past project completion history on RERA
  • Failing to document all communications with the developer for potential complaints
  • Missing the limitation period for filing a RERA complaint after discovering a grievance
  • Not understanding the difference between carpet area and super built-up area in the agreement
FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

Does RERA apply to all projects?

RERA applies to all residential and commercial projects that exceed 500 square metres of land area or have more than 8 units. Projects that have already received a completion certificate before RERA implementation are exempt. However, ongoing projects without a completion certificate were required to register within a specified timeframe.

Can I get a refund under RERA?

Yes. If a developer fails to deliver possession by the agreed date, or if there is a fundamental deficiency in the project that was not disclosed, buyers can seek a full refund along with interest. The RERA authority or appellate tribunal can order refunds with interest calculated from the date of payment to the date of refund.

Is RERA the same in every state?

While RERA is a central Act, each state has the authority to frame its own rules for implementation. This means there can be variations in procedural aspects, fee structures, and certain provisions across states. However, the core protections for buyers remain consistent as they are part of the central legislation.

What if the developer is not registered under RERA?

Operating without RERA registration is a punishable offence. Developers who market or sell units without registration face a penalty of up to 10% of the estimated project cost, and continued non-compliance can lead to imprisonment of up to 3 years. Buyers should report unregistered projects to their state RERA authority immediately.
