OPINION HOUSING POLICY

India’s Real Estate Doesn’t Have a Supply Problem. It Has a Trust Problem.

Why 60 million homes haven’t solved the affordability question — and what actually needs to change.

Rohit Kumar Housing Economist, Delhi
March 25, 2026 12 min read Updated Q1 2026

Key Takeaways

  • India has built 60 million homes under PMAY, yet housing affordability has worsened in 7 of the top 10 cities
  • The trust deficit between buyers and developers remains the single biggest barrier to market efficiency
  • RERA improved transparency but hasn’t solved the fundamental incentive misalignment in pre-sales
  • Affordable housing needs institutional rental supply, not just ownership-driven subsidies
  • Until developers are held to the same accountability standards as listed companies, the trust gap will persist
01

The Supply Myth

Every few months, a new government announcement celebrates another milestone in housing construction. Sixty million homes sanctioned. Forty million completed. The numbers are real, and they are impressive in isolation. But I have spent the better part of two decades studying India’s housing markets, and I believe we are asking the wrong question entirely. The issue was never whether India could build enough homes. The issue is whether the homes being built are the ones people actually need, in the places they actually live, at prices they can actually afford.

The answer, overwhelmingly, is no. Walk through the outskirts of any major Indian city and you will find row after row of completed apartment blocks sitting largely empty. These are not failed projects in the traditional sense — they were built to spec, delivered on time in some cases, and priced at what the market was supposed to bear. They failed because they were built for a buyer who exists on a spreadsheet but not in reality: someone willing to commute ninety minutes each way, without reliable public transit, to a flat that technically qualifies as “affordable” but costs more than what a middle-income family can service after accounting for transportation, schooling, and the daily cost of living far from economic centres.

60M homes built under PMAY — yet affordability has worsened

In my experience, supply-side interventions fail when they ignore demand-side realities. We have optimised for unit count rather than livability. Developers build where land is cheap, not where jobs are plentiful. Subsidies incentivise construction volume, not community infrastructure. The result is a paradox that should trouble every policymaker: we have simultaneously too many homes and not enough homes. The surplus is in the wrong places; the shortage is in the right ones. Until we stop treating housing as a construction problem and start treating it as a urban planning problem, the numbers will keep climbing while the crisis deepens.

02

The Trust Deficit

I have sat across the table from hundreds of prospective homebuyers over the years, and the single emotion that dominates every conversation is fear. Not excitement, not aspiration — fear. Fear that the project will be delayed by three years. Fear that the flat they receive will look nothing like the model apartment. Fear that hidden charges will inflate the final cost by twenty to thirty percent beyond the quoted price. Fear that after handing over their life savings, they will have no meaningful recourse if things go wrong. This fear is not irrational. It is earned, and it is the single largest impediment to a functioning housing market in India.

What I find most striking is how the industry has normalised this dysfunction. In what other sector would a customer pay eighty percent of the cost upfront, wait three to five years for delivery, and have virtually no enforceable guarantee on quality? We would never accept this from an automobile manufacturer, an electronics company, or even a restaurant. Yet in real estate — the largest financial commitment most Indian families will ever make — we have collectively decided that broken promises are just how business works.

Issue Buyer Impact Current Fix What’s Actually Needed
Delayed Delivery EMI + rent burden for years beyond promised date RERA penalty clauses Escrow-linked milestone payments with automatic compensation
Quality Gaps Substandard materials, water seepage, structural issues within months 5-year defect liability under RERA Independent third-party quality audits before handover
Hidden Charges Final cost 20–30% higher than quoted price Carpet area pricing mandate All-inclusive pricing with legally binding cost caps
Title Issues Disputed ownership, legal challenges post-purchase Title insurance (rare) Mandatory title insurance backed by government guarantee
Post-Sale Service Unresponsive developer after possession, poor maintenance RWA formation Developer-funded maintenance escrow for first 5 years

RERA was an important step, and I want to be clear that I am not dismissing its contribution. Project registration, carpet area standardisation, and escrow requirements have all moved the needle. But RERA addressed the symptoms of mistrust without curing the underlying disease. Developers still operate under a fundamentally misaligned incentive structure where they benefit from collecting money early and delivering late. Until we change the economics that reward this behaviour, regulatory patches will continue to fall short.

03

The Pre-Sale Problem

The pre-sale model is the beating heart of Indian real estate, and it is also the source of most of its pathologies. In my assessment, no single structural feature of this market does more damage to buyer confidence than the practice of selling homes that do not yet exist, using money from those sales to fund the construction, and leaving the buyer exposed to every risk along the way. The developer benefits from cheap capital. The buyer bears the construction risk, the timeline risk, the quality risk, and the market risk. This is not a partnership — it is a transfer of liability disguised as a transaction.

72% of residential projects in India are sold before completion

Consider what happens in practice. A developer launches a project, sells seventy percent of the units in the first phase, and uses that capital to acquire land for the next project before completing the first. Cash flows are co-mingled across projects despite RERA’s escrow requirements, because enforcement remains inconsistent and the penalties for violations are rarely severe enough to deter the behaviour. The buyer who purchased in phase one is now effectively financing the developer’s expansion, without equity participation, without interest, and often without a firm delivery date.

What I have observed across markets is that the pre-sale model creates a vicious cycle. Because developers can sell before building, they have less incentive to build reputations based on delivered quality. Because buyers have no completed product to inspect, they rely on brochures, model flats, and promises — all of which are designed to sell, not to inform. And because the transaction is essentially irreversible once the agreement is signed, the power asymmetry is baked into the structure. Mature real estate markets around the world have moved away from this model for good reason. India needs to do the same.

04

What Rental Markets Could Fix

I believe one of the most under-discussed dimensions of India’s housing challenge is the near-total absence of institutional rental supply. We have built an entire ecosystem around ownership — tax incentives for home loans, subsidies for first-time buyers, cultural narratives that equate renting with failure — and in doing so, we have trapped millions of families in a binary choice: buy a home you cannot comfortably afford, or live in informal rental arrangements with no security of tenure. There is almost nothing in between.

“

Our obsession with ownership has distorted Indian real estate markets so completely that we have forgotten what housing is actually for: providing people with a stable, dignified place to live — whether they own it or not.

Rohit Kumar Housing Economist

Look at what institutional rental housing has achieved in markets like Germany, Japan, and Singapore. In these countries, renting is not stigmatised — it is a viable, well-regulated housing solution that provides flexibility for workers, stability for families, and liquidity for the market. Professional landlords maintain properties because their business depends on tenant satisfaction, not on a one-time sale. Buildings are designed for durability and ongoing use, not for marketing photographs. And because renters have legal protections and long-term tenure options, they invest in their communities rather than treating every home as a temporary arrangement.

India is beginning to see the early contours of institutional rental supply through government initiatives and a handful of private ventures. But the pace is far too slow, and the policy framework still overwhelmingly favours ownership. What I would argue is this: every rupee spent subsidising home loans for families who would be better served by affordable, well-managed rental housing is a rupee that deepens the problem. We need to decouple the housing question from the ownership question and ask a simpler one — what is the most efficient way to put a roof over every family’s head?

05

The Accountability Gap

Here is a thought experiment I often present at industry forums. Imagine a publicly listed company that consistently misses its delivery commitments by two to four years. That changes the specifications of its product after customers have already paid. That discloses financial information selectively and resists independent audits. How long would that company survive on the stock exchange? The answer is obvious: it wouldn’t. Institutional investors, regulators, and the market itself would impose consequences swiftly and severely. Yet this is precisely how a significant portion of India’s real estate industry operates, and has operated for decades, largely without consequence.

Metric Listed Companies Real Estate Developers
Financial Disclosure Quarterly audited statements, mandatory SEBI filings Project-level disclosure under RERA, often incomplete
Delivery Timeline Penalised by market for missed guidance Delays normalised; penalties rarely enforced at full value
Quality Standards ISO certifications, third-party audits, product liability Self-certified; no independent quality audit requirement
Customer Redressal Consumer courts, ombudsman, regulatory escalation RERA tribunals with multi-year backlogs
Board Governance Independent directors, audit committees, whistleblower policies Typically family-run; minimal governance infrastructure

The gap is stark, and it explains much of the trust deficit. When developers are not held to the same standards as other businesses that handle large amounts of public money, the message to buyers is clear: you are on your own. I have long argued that the path to a healthier real estate market in India runs through corporate governance reform, not just consumer protection regulation. RERA tells developers what they must disclose. But it does not fundamentally change how they are structured, governed, or held accountable.

What would meaningful accountability look like? In my view, it starts with requiring developers above a certain revenue threshold to adopt the same governance standards as listed companies: independent board members, audited financial statements, transparent land bank disclosures, and whistleblower mechanisms. It means creating a public track record system where every developer’s delivery history, quality record, and complaint resolution rate is visible to any prospective buyer. And it means ensuring that the penalties for non-compliance are severe enough to change behaviour, not just severe enough to appear in the statute books.

06

A Way Forward

I am not a pessimist about Indian real estate. The fundamentals — urbanisation, demographic momentum, rising incomes, infrastructure investment — are powerful and real. What I am pessimistic about is our collective willingness to address the structural issues that prevent these fundamentals from translating into outcomes that benefit ordinary homebuyers. We have spent the last decade celebrating supply-side achievements while the lived experience of buying a home in India has improved only marginally, and in some markets, has actually deteriorated.

The path forward requires honesty about what has not worked and courage to try what has not been tried. It requires moving beyond the comfortable narrative that more construction equals more progress, and confronting the uncomfortable reality that the relationship between India’s real estate industry and its customers is fundamentally broken. Trust cannot be legislated into existence. It must be earned, project by project, commitment by commitment, year after year.

The author’s recommendation

1. Shift to completion-based sales: Mandate that at least 50% of units in any project can only be sold after the structure is complete. This reduces buyer risk, forces developers to arrange project finance independently, and ensures that what is sold is what exists — not what is promised.

2. Create a national developer scorecard: Build a publicly accessible platform that tracks every registered developer’s delivery track record, quality complaints, RERA compliance history, and customer satisfaction scores. Let market transparency do what regulation alone cannot.

3. Incentivise institutional rental housing: Redirect a portion of affordable housing subsidies toward building professionally managed rental housing in employment centres. Give rental housing the same tax treatment as ownership and create a regulatory framework that protects both tenants and institutional landlords.

The question is not whether India can build more homes — it demonstrably can, and it will. The question is whether we can build an industry that buyers trust enough to participate in without fear. In my experience, markets do not fix themselves when the power asymmetry is this severe. They require intervention — not more subsidies, not more construction targets, but structural reform that realigns incentives, enforces accountability, and treats the homebuyer not as a source of cheap capital, but as the customer they are supposed to be. That is the change that will actually move the needle on affordability. Everything else is noise.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

Is India really building enough homes?

In pure numbers, yes. India has built over 60 million homes under PMAY and related schemes. But the problem isn’t volume — it’s type and location. A large share of new supply is concentrated in peripheral areas with poor connectivity, and much of it is priced above what genuinely affordable buyers can manage. Meanwhile, central urban areas where jobs are concentrated face persistent undersupply. The mismatch between where homes are built and where people need them remains the central issue.

Has RERA solved the trust problem?

Partially. RERA has improved transparency around project registration, carpet area disclosure, and delivery timelines. Buyers now have a formal grievance mechanism that did not exist before. But fundamental issues remain: enforcement is inconsistent across states, penalties are often too light to deter violations, and the pre-sale model that transfers risk to buyers is still the industry norm. RERA addressed symptoms — the structural incentive misalignment between developers and buyers persists and requires deeper reform.

What can buyers do to protect themselves?

Start with thorough due diligence: verify RERA registration, check the developer’s delivery track record on previous projects, and read the agreement of sale carefully before signing. Insist on carpet area clarity and verify land title through an independent lawyer — never rely solely on the developer’s documentation. Join or form a buyers’ association for collective bargaining power, and document every promise made by the sales team in writing. Where possible, prefer projects that are nearing completion over early-stage launches, even if the price premium is slightly higher.

Rohit Kumar Housing Economist, Delhi

Rohit Kumar is a housing economist based in Delhi with over two decades of experience studying India’s residential real estate markets. He has advised state governments on housing policy, contributed to NITI Aayog working groups on urban development, and published extensively on affordability, market structure, and regulatory design. His work focuses on the intersection of market incentives, institutional design, and buyer outcomes in emerging economy housing sectors.
